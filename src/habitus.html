Habitus is a tool to practice gesture drawing.

With the tool, you can draw the pose of the mannequin. You can select your own poses, draw with a timer on and break down complex poses. A gesture drawing is a laying in of the action, form, and pose of a model/figure. Typical situations involve an artist drawing a series of poses taken by a model in a short amount of time.

The animations are created by manipulating a SVG with Javascript. GSAP is used for these animations as well as for the User Interface.

Workflow

The character is created in Illustrator and was based on a well-known model. The character was then split-up per body part and labeled accordingly. Below you see what that looks like in Illustrator layers.

We can animate each body part separately because of this process. Through GSAP we can transform, scale, move and rotate the character to create smooth animations.

Visuals

Icons

There was a selection of icons curated for each feature. These also had to work together. When I got to the handpicked keyword, under “select”, I thought of the concept to use hands.

Because if you would adjust the mannequin normally, you would do it with your hands as well.

Breakdown

Abstacting the human physique

Each version of the hand didn’t feel right. They felt out of place and too stylized. Which made them distract from the more important feature. As I brainstormed more, I thought of abstracting the icons further. They needed to become simpler.

I drew inspiration from this drawing practice in which you break down the character you are drawing into simple shapes. Each feature was then reimagined into simple shapes, which receive meaning through motion

Breakdown

But do they work?

After testing the UI along with these icons, I came to the conclusion that the abstract icons were to abstract. They didn’t add something and therefore took away clarity.

The icons were replaced with new icons. I went back to the hand icons. As we would interact with the doll with our hands, we can use the hand icons here to interact.
